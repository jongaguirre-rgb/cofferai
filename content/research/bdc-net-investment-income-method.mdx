## Overview

Business Development Companies (BDCs) are a unique asset class that sit at the intersection of fixed income and private equity. Modeling net investment income (NII) — the primary driver of dividend sustainability and valuation — requires understanding the mechanics of a leveraged credit portfolio. This note documents the analytical framework I apply to every BDC I cover.

## What NII Actually Measures

NII is the BDC's operating earnings from its investment portfolio, calculated as:

**NII = Total Investment Income − Operating Expenses − Interest Expense**

Total investment income includes:
- Interest income on floating-rate debt (the largest component, typically 70–85%)
- Fee income (origination fees, amendment fees, prepayment premiums)
- Dividend income from equity co-investments
- PIK income (payment-in-kind accruals — non-cash but recognized)

<Callout type="warning" title="PIK Income Scrutiny">
PIK (payment-in-kind) income is recognized as earned but received in additional securities rather than cash. Heavy PIK concentration (>15% of NII) is a yellow flag — it can mask underlying borrower stress and inflates coverage ratios without improving liquidity.
</Callout>

## Step 1: Build the Portfolio Yield Model

The starting point is reconstructing the weighted average yield on the debt portfolio:

**Portfolio Yield = Σ(Investment FV × Effective Yield) / Total Portfolio FV**

For floating-rate portfolios (the vast majority of BDC portfolios), effective yield = base rate (SOFR) + spread. I model three components:

1. **Current SOFR:** Use the forward curve for the forecast period
2. **Portfolio Spread:** Back-calculate from disclosed yield data and SOFR levels at time of origination; apply a floor on SOFR floors (typically 1–2% in credit agreements)
3. **Yield Drag from Non-Accruals:** Non-accrual investments contribute zero interest income despite their balance. Adjust total portfolio yield downward by: `Non-Accrual % × Average Portfolio Yield`

## Step 2: Model Operating Expenses

BDC expenses have two components: the management fee structure and G&A / interest expense.

**Management Fees:** Most BDCs charge 1.0–1.75% of gross assets (not net assets). This is an important distinction — management fees scale with leverage, creating an incentive for BDCs to lever up.

- Base management fee: % × average gross assets
- Incentive fee Part I (income): Typically 20% of pre-incentive NII above a preferred return hurdle (~7% annualized). Some BDCs have "lookback" provisions that limit this.
- Incentive fee Part II (capital gains): 20% of cumulative realized + unrealized capital gains above cost (usually calculated annually)

<Callout type="insight" title="Incentive Fee Watch">
Focus on total management + incentive fees as a percentage of NII. A BDC paying >35% of NII in fees is structurally disadvantaged relative to peers charging 25–28%. Fee drag directly reduces dividend capacity.
</Callout>

**Interest Expense:** BDCs fund their portfolios with a mix of:
- Revolving credit facilities (floating rate, typically SOFR + 175–225 bps)
- Unsecured notes (fixed rate, 5–8 year maturities)
- Baby bonds (exchange-listed, fixed rate)

I model weighted average cost of debt as: `Σ(Facility Balance × Rate) / Total Debt`

## Step 3: Validate Against Regulatory Capital

BDCs are regulated under the Investment Company Act of 1940 and must maintain a debt-to-equity ratio of ≤2.0x (asset coverage ratio ≥150%). The coverage ratio constrains dividend policy and leverage:

**Asset Coverage = Total Assets / Total Debt**

Most well-managed BDCs target 1.65–1.8x coverage (debt/equity of 0.56–0.61x) to maintain a buffer above the regulatory minimum. In my models, I stress this ratio in the bear case by assuming:
1. A 10–15% decline in portfolio fair values
2. The resulting covenant headroom contraction
3. Any required deleveraging that would reduce NII

## Step 4: NII Per Share and Dividend Coverage

Once I have aggregate NII, I convert to per-share:

**NII/Share = Total NII / Weighted Average Shares Outstanding**

Dividend coverage is then straightforward:

**Coverage Ratio = NII/Share / Quarterly Dividend**

I require a minimum 105% coverage in the base case; 95% coverage triggers a downgrade of the dividend sustainability rating. Historical averages for investment-grade BDCs run 108–115%.

## Common Modeling Mistakes

1. **Ignoring PIK accruals:** Treating all interest income as cash-equivalent overstates earnings quality
2. **Static SOFR:** BDC earnings are highly rate-sensitive; always sensitize to ±100bps SOFR moves
3. **Gross vs. Net Asset Base:** Management fees on gross assets grow with leverage — this is a non-linear relationship
4. **Ignoring the lookback:** Incentive fee lookback provisions significantly reduce fee drag in up-cycles; include them
5. **Year-end NAV as denominator:** Use weighted average shares for per-share calculations, not period-end
