## Fund Overview

The USV 2004 fund is a canonical example of vintage-driven venture capital outperformance. Raised at the trough of the post-dot-com era with $125M in committed capital, the fund deployed into a market cleared of speculative excess — accessing exceptional entrepreneurs at historically low valuations.

Fred Wilson and Brad Burnham founded Union Square Ventures in 2003 with a clear, differentiated thesis: invest in large networks of engaged users, differentiated through user experience, and defensible through network effects. The 2004 vintage was the vehicle that proved the thesis.

## Investment Thesis at Time of Deployment

The USV thesis in 2004 focused on three structural tailwinds:
1. **Broadband penetration:** Household broadband reaching 30%+ in the U.S., enabling richer web experiences
2. **Open source infrastructure:** Linux, MySQL, Apache reducing startup infrastructure costs by 10x vs. 1999
3. **Network effect platforms:** The emerging hypothesis that social connectivity was a defensible moat, not just a feature

<Callout type="insight" title="Contrarian Entry Point">
The 2004 vintage is particularly notable because many institutional LPs had dramatically reduced or eliminated VC allocations after 2000–2002 losses. USV raised from a small, high-conviction LP base — creating capacity constraints that paradoxically ensured discipline.
</Callout>

## Why This Vintage Outperformed

Three factors explain the exceptional performance:

**1. Entry Timing:** The 2004–2009 deployment window captured companies at seed and Series A stages before institutional capital flooded early-stage venture post-2010. Entry valuations of $3–25M for companies that went public at $1B+ generated structurally high multiples.

**2. Network Effect Thesis Precision:** USV's focus on network effect businesses was not retroactively applied — it was the stated thesis from Fund I. This intellectual coherence meant the portfolio was systematically weighted toward businesses with defensible moats.

**3. Portfolio Concentration:** With 20 positions and ~$6M average check, the fund was concentrated enough to generate meaningful ownership (typically 15–25% at entry) in winners, allowing large absolute dollar returns on IPO exits.

## Key Risk Factors and Lessons

<Callout type="warning" title="Survivorship Bias">
Analysis of exceptional vintage funds carries inherent survivorship bias. The 15+ investments that did not contribute meaningfully to returns consumed capital and management attention. The fund's success is attributable to three to five outliers — a common but under-appreciated feature of power-law VC return distributions.
</Callout>

1. **Replication risk:** The conditions enabling this vintage — cleared market, low valuations, nascent social web — are not replicable. Subsequent USV funds have delivered strong but materially lower returns
2. **LP concentration:** The fund's small LP base ($125M) was a feature, not a bug — it allowed for concentrated ownership
3. **GP continuity:** Wilson and Burnham's continued involvement through the full investment lifecycle (2004–2015+) was critical for portfolio support
