## Company Overview

Stripe is the dominant payments infrastructure provider for internet businesses, processing an estimated $1.4T in total payment volume (TPV) in 2025. Founded in 2010 by Patrick and John Collison, the company has compounded from a developer-friendly payment API into a comprehensive financial operating system for businesses.

This analysis evaluates Stripe's competitive positioning, unit economics, and potential valuation range ahead of an anticipated public market debut.

## Business Model

Stripe's revenue model is primarily volume-based:

- **Core payments:** 2.9% + $0.30 per successful card transaction (blended effective rate ~2.0% after enterprise pricing)
- **Stripe Billing / Revenue Recognition:** SaaS subscription management
- **Stripe Capital:** Merchant cash advances (spreads on loan book)
- **Stripe Atlas / Tax / Treasury:** Platform services (recurring revenue)

Take rate on TPV has compressed from ~2.5% (2015) to an estimated ~1.4% (2025) as enterprise mix has grown, but absolute revenue growth has more than compensated.

Stripe's defensible moat rests on three pillars:

**1. Developer Distribution:** The Stripe API is embedded in an estimated 1M+ businesses globally. Switching costs are high — payment integrations touch checkout flows, subscription logic, reconciliation systems, and financial reporting. A migration to a competing platform typically requires 3–6 months of engineering time.

**2. Data Network Effects:** Processing $1.4T in annual TPV generates proprietary fraud detection data that improves with scale. Stripe Radar (fraud ML model) has a fundamentally lower false positive rate than competitors at equivalent fraud catch rates — a direct function of training data volume.

**3. Platform Expansion:** Each new product (Atlas, Treasury, Capital, Tax) increases switching costs and ARPU. Average revenue per enterprise customer has grown from an estimated $180K (2020) to $400K+ (2025).

<Callout type="insight" title="The Infrastructure Bet">
Stripe is not primarily a payments company — it is the AWS for financial services. The payments processing business funds the build-out of a horizontal financial infrastructure platform that could eventually power banking, lending, and treasury management for millions of businesses.
</Callout>

**Bear Case ($70/share equivalent):** Revenue growth decelerates to 10% as competition from Adyen and PayPal Braintree intensifies in enterprise; margin expansion stalls; multiple compresses to 8x forward revenue.

**Base Case ($115/share):** 17–20% revenue CAGR sustained through 2026; EBITDA margin expands to 20%+ as platform businesses scale; IPO at 12x forward revenue consistent with Adyen's public trading range.

**Bull Case ($191/share):** Platform adjacencies (Stripe Capital, Treasury) contribute $4B+ in revenue at higher margins; international expansion accelerates; 18x forward revenue reflects premium for best-in-class fintech infrastructure.

## Key Risks

<Callout type="warning" title="Primary Risk Factors">
1. **Regulatory:** Payment processing is heavily regulated globally. EU interchange fee regulation, data localization laws, and potential antitrust scrutiny on network rules pose compliance risk.

2. **Competition:** Adyen, PayPal/Braintree, and domestic champions (Razorpay in India, Paymob in MENA) are formidable in specific geographies. Stripe's global ambitions face localization challenges.

3. **Credit risk (Stripe Capital):** The embedded lending business introduces credit cycle exposure not present in pure-play processing. A consumer credit downturn could generate unexpected losses.

4. **IPO timing:** Stripe has passed on multiple IPO windows. Prolonged private status increases secondary market illiquidity and may affect employee retention.
</Callout>
